Mt. Tarawera

Lying 24 kilometres south of Rotorua, Mt. Tarawera is a lava dome volcano consisting of three prominent dome peaks with a central fissure, Mt. Tarawera is responsible for the largest eruption in New Zealand in the last 500 years.

Formation

18, 000 years ago the first eruptions that created what we now know as Mt. Tarawera began. These eruptions were (and still are) caused by the Pacific plate spreading (due to mantle convection currents) and subducting under the Australian plate. As the Pacific plate subducts, it rubs against the Australian plate, producing heat through friction, this heat melts parts of the plate, forming magma, this magma then rises through the crust and, in this case, into the Taupo Volcanic Zone and subsequently to Mt. Tarawera.

For a clearer picture of how the plates interacted, and in that process how Mt. Tarawera and the Taupo Volcanic Zone was formed, see GNS Science's video here.

Mt. Tarawera itself is a series of lava domes, the early lava domes are all covered in rhyolitic (a rock when molten is very viscous) debris, meaning that the early domes were likely formed by slow, viscous rhyolitic lava flows. These domes may either have been formed by eruptions slowly building up over the years or by the rhyolitic lava covering the vents and causing them to swell and inflate from inside. The later domes, and most prominent are thought to have been formed in an eruption around about a thousand years ago, once again these domes were formed with rhyolitic lava. The 1886 eruption of Mt. Tarawera was different to previous eruptions, the 1886 was a scoria (basalt based rock) eruption, rather than a rhyolitic eruption, that blasted out a roughly 17 kilometre long fissure along the three main peaks.
