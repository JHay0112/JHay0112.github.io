Hazards

If Mt. Tarawera were to erupt again there would be devastation in the area, that’s a given. But, what isn’t a given is the type of eruption, While Mt. Tarawera has been rhyolitic for most of its recorded history, the most recent eruption in 1886 was a drastic change from this, Mt. Tarawera erupted scoria and basalt.

Rhyolite

If, next time Mt. Tarawera erupts, it were to erupt rhyolite we’d be facing an eruption with huge volumes of lava and a very violent eruption. Luckily we’d likely have plenty of time to evacuate and prepare as rhyolitic eruptions give off many warning signs, long before the eruption event itself. Including earthquakes, ground deformation, lake size changes and lake water level changes. Hopefully because of this time we’d have to prepare, loss would be at minimum.

Basalt

A basalt eruption however would have very little lava compared to a rhyolitic eruption. But, a basalt eruption would have very little warning, and very little time to evacuate the area. In the case of the 1886 eruption, it wasn’t till an hour before the eruption itself that any noticeable earthquakes occured. Because of this, loss of life may be greater.

Regardless of the type of eruption, most of the ensuing effects would be the same, just on a greater scale if the eruption was a rhyolitic eruption. Some of the effects would be:

  • Pyroclastic Flows
  • Lahars
  • Lava Flows
  • Hot Avalanches
  • Hydrothermal Eruptions
  • Volcanic Gases
  • Forest Fires
  • Flooding
  • Earthquakes
